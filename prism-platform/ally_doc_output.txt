Ally — The Engine, End to End
One platform: associative memory + adversarial reasoning. The architecture, what it enables, why it’s differentiated, and the path to market.
Unusual Insights Inc. · working name “Ally” · internal / investor-credible · v1 · June 2026. Not legal advice.
The premise
Ally is one engine with two faculties. A biomimetic, associative-temporal 
memory
 that holds the whole person across time, and an adversarial, multi-agent 
reasoning
 layer that thinks over what the memory holds. Most AI is one or the other: a stateless model that reasons but forgets, or a retrieval store that remembers but doesn’t reason. Our differentiation is the composition — memory supplies the grounded substrate, the reasoning engine works over it, and together they do what neither does alone. One governing rule holds across every deployment: 
gate the patient-facing act, never the AI’s thinking.
 The engine reasons freely and tells the licensed human exactly what it concludes; only a licensed human performs the regulated act.
The architecture, in one view
Faculty
What it is
What it produces
Memory
A biomimetic associative-temporal graph — concepts as nodes, relationships as weighted edges, significance and credibility as dimensions, consolidated over time. The team calls it the Hippocampus.
A living, longitudinal model of the whole person that persists across every conversation — the grounded substrate.
Reasoning
A model-agnostic, multi-agent adversarial layer — proposer, skeptic, arbiter — with enforced grounding and round-by-round argument tracking.
A calibrated conclusion with its dissent and its can’t-miss flag, plus an auditable record of how it was reached.
The two faculties are not bolted together — they are wired. The reasoning agents are grounded in the memory graph (plus the live chart or case record), not in a one-shot prompt. That single design choice is the whole thesis: 
the engine reasons over a persistent, longitudinal record of the actual person, not a stateless guess.
The capabilities — all of them
Six verbs. The first five are the memory faculty; the sixth is the reasoning faculty running on top of them.
Remembers. 
Persistent, associative, significance-weighted, consolidated. A hippocampus, not a session — it holds the whole person across time, unprompted.
Understands. 
Builds a structured model of the person, infers the connections between events, and surfaces cross-signals a single conversational thread would miss.
Learns. 
Updates and revises; learns the person’s own baseline; holds competing accounts as flagged divergence rather than overwriting them; and is taught directly by the clinician.
Cares. 
Attentive continuity, real-time risk escalation, and respect for consent and locks — framed honestly as bounded support, not sentience, and never a substitute for human connection.
Collaborates. 
A bidirectional team capability: it brings depth to the clinician, and the clinician sets the boundaries that reshape it. Neither tool replaces the other; they work as one team.
Reasons. 
The adversarial engine working over the memory graph — proposer, skeptic, arbiter — surfacing the can’t-miss alternative a single anchored model overlooks. Clinician-facing only.
Under the hood — the mechanisms
Memory
Reinforcement & temporal decay. 
Weight rises with recurrence (“reinforced 47 times across 12 conversations”) and fades without it.
Short-to-long consolidation. 
A working model passes through a consolidation phase into a durable long-term track — a sleep-analog process.
Significance & credibility weighting. 
Two independent axes; competing accounts are held side by side, low-credibility items kept provisional.
Concept & connection weighting. 
Co-mentions are promoted to tracked edges after reinforcement — and significance and connection do not require disclosure: it can infer a bridge to content never stated outright.
Concept replacement & flagged divergence. 
Replace when a fact is superseded; flag when an account contradicts the record.
Reasoning
Composable agents. 
Arbitrary epistemic roles — each a real reasoning stance, not a tone.
Model-agnostic orchestration. 
Any agent runs on any model; for regulated data, BAA-covered endpoints only.
Enforced grounding + anti-fabrication. 
Every claim cites the corpus; a fabricated citation is penalized.
Argument-state tracking (the flow sheet). 
Every claim — raised, addressed, dropped, resolved — tracked and exportable. The auditable reasoning ledger a CMO or regulator can inspect.
Independent adjudicator that explains. 
Scores and explains why the logic fails — not a lazy fallacy label.
Why everything is better together
This is the hinge of the whole platform. 
Memory makes the reasoning trustworthy: 
the adversarial engine is safe in medicine precisely because it reasons over a grounded, longitudinal record instead of a stateless guess — and that transparency is also what keeps it on the non-device side of the FDA’s line, because the clinician can independently review the basis. 
Reasoning makes the memory safe and actionable: 
it interrogates the record, holds contradictions objectively, and surfaces the can’t-miss the record alone would never volunteer.
Neither half is the product on its own. A memory with no reasoning is a filing cabinet. Reasoning with no memory is a brilliant stranger who forgets you the moment you leave the room. 
The marriage of the two is the part only we have.

Scores the registry daily by acuity; flags who is trending worse by the model’s own criteria, and who has gone silent.

Adversarial differential. 
Catches the bipolar-II before an antidepressant — the can’t-miss alternative a single anchored model would overlook.
Between-session companion.

Documentation & billing integrity.

In litigation (the second frontier)
Causation analysis. 
Adversarial temporal reasoning over the case record, exhibits, and depositions.
Vulnerability map. 
The flow sheet becomes a map of which arguments survive scrutiny and which get dropped — attorney-facing.
Why it’s differentiated
It cannot be a prompt on a stateless model. 
A general-purpose LLM forgets between contacts, can’t hold the longitudinal graph, can’t reason over contradiction, and can’t coordinate with a care team.
The moat is the composition. 
Persistent associative graph + adversarial grounding + argument tracking + the biomimetic mechanisms, wired together — not any one of them alone.

The path to commercialization
Two surfaces, one engine
Patient companion (non-clinical): 
gets someone started, holds them between sessions, listens to the visit with consent, builds the memory — always discloses it is AI, never diagnoses or directs treatment. 
Clinician copilot (supplementary support): 
the full clinical brain — assessment, recommendations, risk, consult briefs, documentation, billing integrity — surfaced to the licensed clinician, who decides and acts. The firewall: clinical reasoning flows to the clinician; only clinician-authorized content flows back to the patient.
The wedge: CoCM behavioral health
Reimbursement-native. 
Billed monthly per enrolled patient and additive to FQHC PPS — the buyer makes money adopting it.
Clinician-led by federal design. 
The PCP bills, the care manager is licensed, the consultant directs from behind the glass — the regulatory safe harbor comes free.
Warm distribution. 
Castle, Damien, and the HOPE Network FQHCs, stacked on the PrEP / 340B 
telehealth
 base already running. Not a cold start.
Timely pain. 
As of January 1, 2026, FQHCs must bill the individual CoCM codes (99492–99494; bundled G0512 retired; APCM add-ons G0568–G0570 as the alternate route) — a transition that broke billing workflows and is itself a wound to sell into.
The numbers (illustrative, Medicare basis): 
≈ $135 per enrolled patient / month (subsequent); ≈ $486K/year from a single 300-patient panel, additive to PPS; roughly 2× better outcomes than usual care — the citable figure. A few dozen FQHC programs reach $5M ARR; the constraint is distribution and ACV, not market size. 
Verify CY2026 PFS and per-payer rates before any external use.
Sequencing

— reimbursable, lowest regulatory risk, fastest yes.

in a permissive state (Texas, Utah, Tennessee). Avoid Illinois, Nevada, Missouri for anything patient-facing; jurisdiction follows the patient, not HQ.
Litigation (Crux) as the deliberate second act 
— adversarial-led, bigger and more crowded, no warm channel yet. Same IP, sequenced — not split.
